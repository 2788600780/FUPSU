Abstract. Private Set Union (PSU) allows two parties to compute the union of their pri-
vate sets without revealing any additional information—in particular, it hides their common
elements (the intersection). Although recent years have seen significant progress under the
semi-honest model, resulting in several efficient two-party PSU protocols, notable gaps re-
main: (1) some prior works model the semi-honest PSU functionality inaccurately, and (2)
practical and scalable maliciously secure protocols are still lacking, except when relying on
heavy generic techniques (e.g., FHE, GMW, or general purpose NIZK).
In this paper, we address these issues directly and summarize our contributions as follows:
 1. We revisit the formal definition of PSU, covering both the standard one-sided function-
    ality (where only one party receives the output) and the two-sided variant (where both
    parties receive the output), refuting several flawed claims from prior work, and show that
    the notion of “during-execution leakage” was not well-defined in the literature, since the
    “enhanced” functionality is actually equivalent to the standard one.
 2. We show how one of the fastest semi-honest protocols can be strengthened against
    malicious senders with a simple ad-hoc modification, while preserving its efficiency and
    simplicity.
 3. As our main result, we present the first practical, concretely efficient, and maliciously
    secure two-sided PSU protocol, achieving at least a quadratic improvement over prior
    work. Along the way, we also resolve the challenge of assuring honest behavior for the
    hash-to-curve function in the PSU context—a task generally regarded as impractical due
    to the non-algebraic nature of the hash function.
 4. We implement both protocols and compare them with existing schemes. Our experiments
    demonstrate that our maliciously secure protocols are only 1.1−2.4× slower than the
    most efficient semi-honest protocols in the literature.

1   Introduction

Private set union (PSU) is a cryptographic protocol that enables two parties to compute the union
of their private sets while leaking no additional information. A subtlety here is that even the set
difference is revealed as part of the output, the intersection remains hidden.
    PSU has a wide range of important applications. For instance, it supports privacy-preserving
identity management systems, enabling private-ID functionality [BKM+ 20]. It can facilitate joint
cyber risk assessment and management by allowing organizations to collaboratively construct and
share IP blacklists or vulnerability data without disclosing sensitive details [HLS+ 16]. In private
database systems, PSU enables full joins across multiple data sources while keeping the underlying
records private [KRTW19]. It also plays a role in association rule learning [KC04], where it helps
securely identify patterns across distributed datasets, and in joint graph computations [BS05],
where parties can compute properties of a graph without exposing their individual components.
In the domain of network security, PSU allows for the aggregation of multi-domain network events
[BSMD10], making collective traffic analysis possible without revealing proprietary information.
    Given these diverse applications, the design of efficient, scalable, and secure PSU protocols has
become a critical research focus. In recent years, notable progress has been made in the two-party
setting: Kolesnikov, Rosulek, Trieu, and Wang [KRTW19] introduced a practical oblivious transfer
(OT)-based PSU framework secure against semi-honest adversaries, which has since been optimized
and extended by numerous follow-up works [GMR+ 21,JSZ+ 22,ZCL+ 23,BPSY23,JSZG24], leading
to significant efficiency gains.
    Nevertheless, despite this progress, two fundamental limitations remain in the existing litera-
ture, which we outline below.
Semi-Honest Setting. Jia et al. [JSZG24] observe that the standard PSU functionality might
leak the intersection to the receiver, because who could infer it from the received union and its own
input. They further argue that this leakage might be critical if the receiver aborts early, and they
denote it “during-execution leakage.” To mitigate this, they propose an “enhanced” functionality
for stronger security. We find these claims unconvincing, and the enhanced functionality comes
with additional overhead. This motivates us to ask:

    Does standard functionality actually allow such leakage? More broadly, what is the proper
                                       definition for PSU?

Malicious Setting. While the semi-honest setting has seen rapid progress, only two works, to the
best of our knowledge, have attempted to address malicious security.
    The first, by Hazay and Nissim [HN12], observes that it is trivial to make PSU secure against
malicious adversaries by simply having the sender transmit its items to the receiver in the clear.
Although this may sound counterintuitive, it reflects the fact that a malicious receiver is very
powerful in the ideal world: by submitting random inputs to the functionality, it can effectively
extract the sender’s items. To account for this, they put forward the functionality with two-sided
output for malicious security (see Section 2 for an intuitive explanation).
    In the same work, they also proposed a protocol with quadratic (computational and commu-
nication) overhead O(κ · (nℓ + N )) in the security parameter κ, where ℓ = κ is the item length
and n, N the set size. While feasible in some sense, this approach is impractical—and, more se-
riously, insecure due to flaws in their bucketing technique: precisely speaking, after applying the
bucketing technique, the receiver can tell which buckets contain items lying in the set difference
and which do not, as shown in [HN12, Page 27]. Buckets that do not contain those items still


                                                 3
have a non-negligible chance of holding intersections. For detailed attacks against such bucketing,
see [JSZ+ 22]. 3
    A later work [GHLL22] considered only the standard one-sided functionality with malicious
senders, using fully homomorphic encryption (FHE). Thus, the following important question re-
mains open:

Is there a practical and highly scalable PSU protocol with two-sided output that is secure against
                                      malicious adversaries?

    Regarding the second question above, it may appear natural to assume that a malicious protocol
with two-sided output is far more costly than its semi-honest counterpart. For example, in the case
of private set intersection (PSI)—a closely related problem to PSU—the fastest known malicious
PSI protocols with two-sided output [MPR+ 20, YLS+ 24] still require at least 9 − 110 hours of
computation (with setup) for sets of size 220 , whereas semi-honest PSI can be completed in under
a second (with setup) [RR22]. The gap should be even more striking for PSU, which is generally
considered harder than PSI because it must also hide the intersection. As a result, even the best
semi-honest PSU protocols today still take 76−127 seconds for sets of size 220 [ZCL+ 23, CZZ+ 24,
TBZ+ 25].
    However, we will show, perhaps surprisingly, that a malicious two-sided PSU protocol could be
much simpler and substantially more efficient than two-sided PSI, taking only 184−192 seconds of
computation for sets of size 220 . This is just 1.6−2.4× slower than the state-of-the-art semi-honest
protocols.


1.1    Our Contributions

In this work, we address the questions above through the following contributions.

 – In Section 4, we revisit the PSU functionality, covering both the standard FPSU with one-sided
   output, and the two-sided output version FPSU2 . Along the way, we refute several flawed claims
   from prior work, and show that the notion of “during-execution leakage” was not well-defined
   in the literature—because the so-called “enhanced” functionality is actually equivalent to the
   standard one (Theorem 1).
 – In Section 5, we boost the security of a semi-honest instantiation of FPSU against malicious
   senders with an ad-hoc fix, while retaining its simplicity and efficiency.
 – In Section 6, as our main result, we introduce the first practical and efficient protocol for FPSU2
   in the malicious setting. Our protocol achieves linear complexity in the security parameter,
   significantly improving over the quadratic overhead in existing work [HN12], by leveraging new
   ideas in non-standard ways.
 – In Section 7, we implement both protocols and show that they are only 1.1−2.4× slower than
   the state-of-the-art semi-honest protocols. We highlight the performance results in Section 1.2.

 In this section, we provide a high-level overview of our constructions. To set the stage, we start by

2.1     Definition of Private Set Union
  We begin by recalling the standard PSU functionality with one-sided output below. The func-
tionality FPSU takes as input a set W from the sender P1 and a set V from the receiver P0 , and
returns the union V ∪ W to the receiver.
  Functionality FPSU

      – Wait for a set V = {v1 , . . . } ⊂ {0, 1}ℓ from party P0 .
      – Wait for a set W = {w1 , . . . } ⊂ {0, 1}ℓ from party P1 .
      – If |V | ̸= n or |W | ̸= N , send ⊥ to party P0 and halt.
      – Send V ∪ W to party P0 and halt.
Since the difference W \ V is inherently revealed as part of the output, the only information hidden
from P0 is the intersection V ∩W within its own set V . In other words, P0 should not learn whether
any particular element vi ∈ V belongs to the intersection.
    In their recent work [JSZG24, Section 3.2], Jia et al. claim that P0 can select some “suitable”
hash function to map both W \ V and V into buckets, and then apply an analysis from prior
works [JSZ+ 22] to find the intersection. This claim, however, is flawed: since the intersection V ∩W
is independent of the difference W \ V , it should remain perfectly hidden within V , even when
P0 knows both V and W \ V . A thorough discussion regarding FPSU and its variants is provided
in Section 4.

Towards Malicious Security. To enhance the above protocol with malicious security, it’s natural
to consider using maliciously secure building blocks. However, a challenge arises that we still need
to “bind” these blocks together, otherwise, a malicious receiver could arbitrarily choose its OT
selection bits (i.e., bj in Step 5 above) even the shuffled OPRF and OT are both secure against
malicious adversaries. It is unclear how to do this if we treat them as black boxes.
    Perhaps counter-intuitively, the “binding” is not necessary for malicious security since Hazay
and Lindell [HL10] showed that it’s trivial to secure against a malicious receiver: if the simulator
S samples a random set of size |V | then sends it to FPSU , S would get honest sender’s set W
from FPSU when the item universe is super-polynomially large since the intersection is empty with
overwhelming probability. It further implies that a trivial protocol (that is, P1 directly sends W to
P0 ) realizing FPSU against malicious adversaries, remains insecure against semi-honest adversaries
(as the intersection should be hidden for semi-honest receivers). Please refer to Appendix C for
more detail.
    Given that the fully malicious security for FPSU is trivial, there are two viable options to upgrade
a semi-honest protocol to anything non-trivial: either we consider the security against malicious
sender and semi-honest receiver, or we consider the highly non-trivial functionality with two-sided
output where both parties get the union V ∪ W . We discuss the former in Section 2.3 as a warmup
and the latter in Section 2.4.


2.3   Warmup: PSU with Malicious Sender

 Intuitively, a malicious sender might arbitrarily choose OT messages in such a way that the honest
receiver is unable to retrieve the intended item in W \ V . To mitigate such adversarial behavior,
the simulator S must extract P1 ’s effective input during simulation. Without this extraction, S
cannot appropriately instruct the ideal functionality FPSU to produce a consistent output for P0 .
    If the shuffled OPRF described earlier were secure against malicious adversaries, S would be
able to extract the input straightforwardly. However, the hash function H disrupts the underlying
algebraic structure, making it challenging to achieve malicious security. While it is possible to make
HashDH verifiable and UC-compatible, as shown in 2HashDH [JKK14], its verifiability for mali-
cious security inherently conflicts with the shuffling property essential to the PSU protocol. Other
maliciously secure shuffled OPRFs [MPR+ 20] built from significantly more expensive techniques
which we will discuss in the next section.

senders. Instead of relying on malicious OPRF, we simply modify the Step 5 to:

2.4     Main Result: PSU with Two-Sided Output
  One-sided functionality for PSU is too limited in a malicious setting, as it gives the receiver too
much power. The receiver could potentially learn far more information than it should without being
detected by the honest sender. To mitigate this limitation and rule out trivial protocols, we adopt
the two-sided PSU functionality FPSU2 proposed in [HN12] (shown below), which ensures that the
honest party can detect whether the malicious party behaves in an unusual way. For example, P0
may find it suspicious if P1 inputs a large set that has no intersection with P0 ’s set.

Achieving two-sided functionality FPSU2 is generally more challenging because it requires addressing

Furthermore, the ad-hoc fix described in Section 2.3 no longer works: we have to enforce a check

Potential Building Blocks. The first approach that comes to mind is to leverage the maliciously
secure shuffled and distributed OPRF (sDOPRF) developed by Miao, Patel, Raykova, Seth, and
Yung [MPR+ 20], where both parties learn a sequence of indication bits {bj } of the other party’s
set (similar to what happens in Step 4).
    Unfortunately, while this seems like a natural fit, the malicious sDOPRF has a significant
downside: it’s expensive in both computation and communication. The protocol relies on obliviously
evaluating Dodis-Yampolskiy PRF [DY05], which requires Paillier [JL09, CDGS19] or Camenisch-
Shoup encryptions [CS03,MPR+ 20]. Both of them are far less efficient than DH-based approaches.
In fact, the sDOPRF proposed in [MPR+ 20] is at least 195 times slower than the semi-honest
shuffled HashDH. Another drawback of this protocol is that it relies on stronger assumptions than
plain DDH, specifically the q-Diffie-Hellman Inversion assumption [MSK02, BB04] and the Strong
RSA assumption [FO97].
    Recently, there is a follow-up [YLS+ 24] replacing Camenisch-Shoup used in [MPR+ 20] with
SPDZ [DPSZ12]. Thus it still depends on stronger assumptions (q-DHI and lattices) than DDH.
While [YLS+ 24] is “somewhat” more efficient than [MPR+ 20] in the online phase—reducing run-
time from 9 hours to 36 minutes—it requires super heavy and interactive offline computation and
communication (29 GB of bandwidth and 110 hours of runtime for sets of size 220 , as we shown
in Table 1).
    Upon closer examination, we find that malicious sDOPRF is overkill for PSU. 5 We will show
that it is sufficient to realize FPSU2 against malicious adversaries using a semi-honest shuffled
HashDH, incorporated into a carefully and innovatively designed protocol.

In this section, however, we argue that the above claims are inaccurate and provide the following
corrections. Specifically, we show that (1) the functionality (and the union it reveals) does not intro-
duce any meaningful leakage, so the bucketing attack [JSZ+ 22] remains effective against [KRTW19];
(2) neither during-execution leakage nor the attack of Guo et al. [GHL+ 22] aligns with the standard
semi-honest security model; and (3) the proposed “enhanced PSU functionality” neither captures
during-execution behavior nor is it well defined.

No Leakage from the Standard Functionality. Here we view “leakage derived from the union”
as equivalent to “leakage from the functionality,” since the PSU functionality FPSU provides no
other interface. Any information an adversary obtains must therefore come from the functionality’s
output, namely the union.
     Recall that the authors of [JSZG24] claim that, given access to a standard PSU functionality, a
receiver can infer information about the intersection as follows. After receiving the union X ∪Y from
FPSU , it forms the two sets X \Y and Y (where X and Y denote the sender’s and receiver’s inputs),
and hashes them into two tables T0 and T1 , respectively, using an “appropriate” hash function h.
They claim that if some bin is empty in T0 but occupied in T1 (e.g., ∃, i, yj such that h(yj ) = i,
T0 [i] = ∅, and T1 [i] = yj ), then one can conclude with good probability that yj ∈ X ∩ Y . The
intuition is that, when hashing X into a table with |X| ≈ |T0 |, empty bins should be rare; thus an
empty bin T0 [i] suggests that some element x ∈ X that is missing from X \Y (i.e., some x ∈ X ∩Y )
should have mapped to i-th bin. If additionally x and yj collide in that bin (as T1 [i] = yj ), there
is a non-negligible probability to infer x = yj . This is essentially the bucketing attack of [JSZ+ 22],
originally used against an earlier PSU protocol of [KRTW19] (denoted KRTW19).
     However, that attack against KRTW19 relies on an implicit assumption that does not hold
in the ideal world of PSU functionalities: namely, that the sender and receiver agree on the same
randomly sampled hash function h. The bucketing attack is probabilistic, with its success proba-
                                     $
bilities taken over the sampling h ← H. In the ideal world, the receiver only observes X \ Y and
Y , and no hash function is sampled by anyone; any probabilities the receiver computes (e.g., the


                                                   15
probability of an empty bin, or the probability that yj ∈ X ∩ Y ) come solely from its own choice of
    $
h ← H and therefore do not reveal information about the intersection. Hence, the attack is invalid
in the ideal world.
    In fact, given that the intersection X ∩ Y of two sets is indeed independent of its difference
X \ Y , the intersection should be perfectly hidden in Y even with the knowledge of X \ Y and Y .
In other words, when X \ Y and Y are fixed, the intersection X ∩ Y still has the full entropy and
can be chosen independently from elements in Y . As a simple example, consider the sender holds
X := {x, 4} where x ∈ [3] and receiver holds Y := {1, 2, 3}. In this case, the adversary (receiver) A
obtains X ∪ Y = {1, 2, 3, 4}, which reveals no information about x except x ∈ [3]. It is clear that
A can only guess x with probability no greater than 1/3, and information-theoretically cannot do
any better.
    Therefore, the standard functionality FPSU is well-defined for semi-honest adversaries and yields
no leakage beyond the output.

During-Execution Leakage Violates Semi-Honest Behavior. In [JSZG24, Section 3.3], the
authors further observed that the protocol in [KRTW19] could let the receiver learn shared items
during protocol execution (i.e., before an abort), rather than only after the protocol completes.
They refer to this kind of leakage as “during-execution leakage.”
   Moreover, to motivate the practical influence of such notion, they observe that such during-
execution leakage can be leveraged by a realistic attack [GHL+ 22] to reveal more information 7 .
   However, we would like to point out that the attack from [GHL+ 22] relies on a malicious receiver
adaptively choosing its input set across many sessions, and therefore does not apply to semi-honest
adversaries. Furthermore, the early-abort of a receiver already violates the standard semi-honest
behavior of parties.

Remark 1. We stress that the early-abort only lets the receiver learn its own output (e.g., part
of the union items, the cardinality of the intersection, etc.) before the protocol completes. This is
fundamentally different from the type of leakage discussed above, and learning the output before
termination should not be considered a security issue in the semi-honest setting.
    As a direction for future work, one could explore hybrid security models that explicitly account
for during-execution behavior and sit between the semi-honest and malicious settings.

4.2    PSU Functionality for Malicious Adversaries

While the standard functionality is well-defined in semi-honest settings as discussed above, the
malicious setting is a bit tricky. For instance, a malicious receiver might leverage the knowledge of
the intersection cardinality and arbitrarily modify its input to extract additional information about
the intersection, by using the attack mentioned in [GHL+ 22]. Though the cardinality is implied
by the union of sets (i.e., it is part of the output of the functionality), the malicious receiver could
abort the protocol as soon as it learns the cardinality.
   However, as pointed out in [HL10, Example 2], PSU functionality against malicious adversaries
can be trivially realized by communicating their sets in the plain, since a malicious receiver can
extract the sender’s entire input by querying the functionality with randomized elements. For
completeness, we provide a detailed discussion in Appendix C. This renders the above attack less
powerful for the PSU context: a rational adversary might prefer not to abort the protocol so it can
completely learn the sender’s set.


Semi-honest Receiver and Malicious Sender. As discussed above and shown in [HL10],
a protocol realizing the standard functionality (Figure 2) secure against malicious adversaries
does not imply security against semi-honest adversaries. Therefore, the best we can hope for this
functionality is the security against a semi-honest receiver and malicious sender (since the security
against a malicious receiver is trivial).

5     Warmup: PSU with Malicious Sender
In this section, we present a simple PSU protocol designed for a semi-honest receiver and a malicious
sender, which is particularly suited for one-sided input PSU scenarios. For a detailed discussion
on the protocol addressing both malicious senders and receivers, we refer the reader to Section 4.2

5.1     Protocol Description
Our protocol builds on the common framework of existing two-party semi-honest PSU protocols
(see Section 2.3), with the key difference in the OT phase: the receiver is now able to prevent a
malicious sender from injecting invalid inputs during OT execution. To achieve this, we require
an AoK for each OT message, guaranteeing that the sender uses consistent inputs throughout the
protocol. In particular, the AoK ensures that P1 knows the exponent k such that H(wi )k = yi . The
full construction of our protocol is given in Figure 5.

that OT typically consists of two steps:

 – Random OT: The sender obtain two random messages (r0 , r1 ), while the receiver obtains rb ,

of Π −1 per encoded item. Importantly, these overheads remain marginal compared to the dominant
cost of scalar multiplications.

A    More Related Works
Kolesnikov, Rosulek, Trieu, and Wang [KRTW19] proposed an efficient two-party PSU protocol
primarily based on symmetric-key techniques. The core building block of their protocol is the
reverse private membership test (RPMT), which is a two-party protocol where a receiver holding
a set V learns whether a given element w from the sender belongs to V , while the sender learns
nothing. In the context of PSU, by invoking RPMT, the receiver obtains a bit b ∈ {0, 1} indicating
the membership of w ∈ V . Subsequently, they perform an OT, allowing the receiver to learn the
value of w if b = 1 and nothing otherwise (b = 0).
    To further optimize performance, [KRTW19] introduced a bucketing technique. However, Jia
et al. [JSZ+ 22] later identified that this technique introduces information leakage. Specifically,
they showed that the bucketing approach based on hashing leaks partial information about the
intersection during execution. In particular, the receiver may infer whether certain bins contain
elements that belong to the intersection. Furthermore, they demonstrated through experiments
that the dummy element solution proposed by [KRTW19] is insufficient to completely eliminate
such leakage.
    Jia et al. [JSZ+ 22] proposed a new PSU protocol that relies on the permute-share functionality
in combination with an OPRF. In their protocol, the sender inputs a permutation π and learns the
receiver’s input set in a permuted order, masked by random values, i.e., {vπ(j) ⊕ rπ(j) }. Meanwhile,
the receiver learns the corresponding mask values in the same permuted order, i.e., {rπ(j) }. The
sender then sends to the receiver the set of masked values: {wi ⊕vπ(1) ⊕rπ(1) , . . . , wi ⊕vπ(n) ⊕rπ(n) }.
If any mask value sπ(j) matches a value obtained from the sender, the receiver learns a bit indicating
that the item at π(j) is part of the intersection. Consequently, this item can be discarded in the
subsequent execution of OT. To further improve efficiency, the authors use a combination of cuckoo
hashing and simple hashing.
    The cuckoo/simple hashing scheme plays a crucial role in designing private set operations.
Garimella, Mohassel, Rosulek, Sadeghian, and Singh [GMR+ 21] utilized the same OT-based ap-
proach and introduced a new building block called the permuted characteristic. This is a two-party
protocol where the sender learns a permutation π of their set W , while the receiver, holding a set
V , obtains a vector e, where ei = 1 if the sender’s π(i)-th item is in Y . Their protocol is built upon
commonly used techniques such as cuckoo/simple hashing, oblivious shuffling, and special-purpose
equality tests.
    More recently, [CSSW25] proposed an efficient PSU protocol that can be instantiated using
any cuckoo/simple hashing-based circuit PSI protocol. In this approach, the circuit PSI protocol
is executed over the PRF values of the original input sets, enabling the sender and receiver to
obtain shares of the intersection indicator bits. They further introduced a functionality called
combine and permute, which allows the receiver to learn the combined bits in a random order.
This randomization enables the subsequent execution of OT to reveal the differences between the
two sets. The idea of leveraging the shared output of circuit PSI has gained increasing attention
and is often referred to as the secret-shared private membership test (SS-PMT) in various PSU
works [LG23, GNT24, DCZB24, JSZG24].
    Although efficient PSU protocols can be constructed using the cuckoo/simple hashing scheme,
their complexity remains super-linear in terms of the input set size. This is due to the need for a
permutation step to break the correspondence between hash bins and elements. To address this
limitation, [ZCL+ 23] proposed the first OT-based PSU protocol with linear computation and com-
munication complexity. Their approach leverages the oblivious key-value store (OKVS) [GPR+ 21]
and introduces a multi-query reverse private membership test (mq-RPMT) that combines both
public-key and symmetric-key techniques. Unlike hashing-based schemes, their protocol eliminates


                                                    31
the need for explicit hashing, as the order-hiding property of OKVS removes the necessity for
computationally expensive permutations.
     Building on this idea, Bienstock, Patel, Seo, and Yeo [BPSY23] further optimized PSU per-
formance by designing a more efficient OKVS. More recently, Chen et al. [CZZ+ 24] developed an
efficient PSU protocol based on a Diffie-Hellman (DH)-based shuffle-OPRF technique. Their ap-
proach enables permutation to be performed directly over DH-PRF values, allowing the protocol
to achieve linear complexity as well.


B     Additional Preliminaries
B.1    Universal Composability
  The Universal Composability (UC) framework [Can01] provides a strong security guarantee by
ensuring that a protocol remains secure even when composed with arbitrary other protocols. In this
model, security is defined by comparing a real-world execution of the protocol with an ideal-world
execution involving a trusted functionality. An adversary interacts with parties in both settings,
and the goal is to construct a simulator that makes these interactions indistinguishable.
    In our security proof, we focus on static corruption, where the adversary chooses which parties

D     The Enhanced PSU Functionality from JSZG24
The functionality introduced in [JSZG24, Figure 11] is recalled in Figure 3, with modifications
highlighted in blue to show the differences from the standard FPSU functionality (Figure 2). For
clarity, we simplify the description by omitting unnecessary notions (e.g., session id, states of each
party).
    This functionality can be interpreted as having a “two-sided” output, except that P1 ’s output is
trivial—it is constant and does not depend on the other party’s input. However, since the function
being evaluated (the union of sets) is deterministic, the simulator only needs to simulate the
transcripts, as the output is determined solely by both parties’ inputs in the semi-honest setting.
As a result, FePSU does not offer any meaningful distinction (even for malicious adversaries) from
FPSU given the following theorem.


                                                    33
Theorem D.1 (Recall from Theorem 1). The standard functionality FPSU defined in Figure 2
is equivalent to the “enhanced” functionality FePSU (recalled in Figure 3) within the UC framework,
against both semi-honest and malicious adversaries. That is, any protocol realizing FPSU can also
realize FePSU , and vice versa.

Proof. We begin by showing how to realize FePSU using FPSU . In the FPSU -hybrid model, this is
simple by having P1 also output ⟨Finished⟩ after sending inputs to FPSU —Remember an interactive
Turing machine (ITM) can always write the subroutine-output tape of the environment Z during
its execution (i.e., sending output to Z). First, when the sender P1 is corrupted, it is trivial since
in both hybrid and ideal games there are no transcripts, and honest party P0 ’s output is identical
in hybrid and ideal games given the same P1 ’s input. To see why this FPSU -hybrid game perfectly
emulates FePSU when P0 is corrupted, let us consider two cases:
 – Semi-honest adversaries. In the hybrid world, P1 always outputs ⟨Finished⟩. The simulator S
   proceeds as follows: when Z gives P0 ’s input, S directly forwards it to FePSU (since a semi-
   honest adversary will never halt or delay the input), and then immediately issues the ⟨Response⟩
   query to FePSU . Because P1 outputs ⟨Finished⟩ in both worlds, P0 receives exactly the same
   result from FPSU and FePSU , and no transcript is exchanged between the parties. Therefore, Z
   cannot distinguish the hybrid from the ideal world.
 – Malicious adversaries. This is a more interestig case: here, P0 could halt and never send its
   input, while P1 still outputs ⟨Finished⟩ in the hybrid world. To handle this, we exploit the fact
   that the malicious receiver is powerful (as discussed in Section 4.2): S first inputs a random
   set into FePSU and then immediately sends the ⟨Response⟩ query to FePSU , without waiting
   for Z’s input to P0 . In this way, P0 actually extracts P1 ’s input. Since P1 outputs ⟨Finished⟩
   in both worlds and no transcript is generated, we only need to ensure P0 ’s view is identical.
   Whenever Z supplies P0 ’s input, S computes the union itself using the extracted set from P1 .
   This matches P0 ’s view exactly in both worlds, so Z has no advantage in distinguishing.
    Conversely, to realize FPSU in the FePSU -hybrid model, it suffices to have P1 not output any-
thing. In other words, P1 never produces ⟨Finished⟩, which matches FPSU ’s behavior. Again, there
is no transcript, so Z sees the same execution in both the hybrid and ideal worlds.
    Thus, we conclude that FPSU and FePSU are equivalent.                                       ⊔
